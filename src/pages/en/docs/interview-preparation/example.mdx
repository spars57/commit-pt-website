## Why do you want to work at this company?

### **What the recruiter is looking for**

Whether you have genuine interest in the company or are just applying generically. They want to see if you’ve researched the company, understand the product, mission, or impact of the work, and if your career goals align with what the company does.

### **How to structure your answer**

1. Show you know the company — technologies, products, impact, initiatives, mission.
2. Connect that to your professional interests — what specifically draws you to this company?
3. Explain how you can add value.

### **Example answer**

What stood out to me about **[company name]** was the work you’re doing in **[product / area / project]**. It feels like a product with real impact, and that’s something I care about.

I also saw you work with **[technology]**, which is an area where I already have experience and really enjoy. So I felt there was a good fit between what you do and the kind of challenges I’m looking for.

At **[previous company]** I worked on **[type of project]** and was involved with **[technology / context]**, so I’m confident I can add value in this role and get up to speed quickly.

### **Important tip**

If you can mention **a specific product or feature**, it significantly improves your answer. For example:

- product features
- system scale
- problems they solve
- impact on users

That shows **real interest**, not just preparation.

## Why do you want to leave your current/previous company?

### **What the recruiter is looking for**

Whether your reason for leaving is professional and healthy, or tied to conflict, lack of commitment, or negative attitudes. They also want to see if what you’re looking for in the next role is something the company can actually offer.

### **How to structure your answer**

1. Acknowledge the value of your current/previous experience — what you learned and the impact you had.
2. Explain the reason for the change in a positive way — growth, new challenges, scale, new responsibilities.
3. Tie that to what you’re looking for now — show the move is a natural step in your career.

### **Example answer**

I’ve had a positive experience at **[company name]** and learned a lot, especially in **[area / technology / type of problem]**.

At the same time, I feel I’m at a stage where I want to take on different challenges, ideally with **more scale, more technical complexity, or more impact on the product**.

That’s why this opportunity appealed to me. It feels like a natural next step to keep growing and put myself in a context where I can develop further.

## What are you looking for in your next role?

### **What the recruiter is looking for**

Your professional motivations and whether what you want is aligned with what the company can offer. It also helps them see if you’re looking for technical growth, product impact, or just a circumstantial change.

### **How to structure your answer**

1. Describe the kind of challenges you’re looking for — scale, impact, technical complexity.
2. Mention the work environment you value — collaboration, engineering quality, autonomy.
3. Connect that to the role you’re applying for.

### **Example answer**

In the next step of my career I’m mainly looking for three things: **interesting technical challenges, real impact on the product, and a strong team I can learn from**.

I like environments where there’s care about engineering quality, where technical decisions are thought through and discussed, and where there’s some autonomy to contribute.

From what I understand about this role, it seems to match those characteristics well, and that’s what attracted me.

## Tell me about a situation where you had a conflict with a colleague

### **What the recruiter is looking for**

Your ability to collaborate, communicate, and resolve conflict within a team. The goal isn’t to avoid conflict, but to see **how you handle it professionally**.

### **How to structure your answer**

1. Briefly explain the context of the conflict.
2. Describe how you approached the situation.
3. Share the outcome and what you learned.

### **Example answer**

I remember a situation where a colleague and I had different views on how to implement an important part of the application.

He was more focused on a solution that could be shipped faster, and I was more concerned about maintaining it in the long run. Instead of taking it personally, we tried to focus the discussion on the right things: the trade-offs of each approach and what made most sense for the context.

In the end we found a balanced solution. What I took from it is that technical disagreement will always exist — the key is being able to discuss it calmly and focus on the problem.

---

## Tell me about a situation where you had to influence someone without direct authority

### **What the recruiter is looking for**

Your ability to **influence decisions without formal authority**, which is very common in engineering teams.

### **How to structure your answer**

1. Set the context of the problem.
2. Explain how you communicated your point of view.
3. Show how you influenced the outcome.

### **Example answer**

There was a time when I felt the way we were managing **[state / architecture / component / flow]** would start to cause problems as the product grew.

Not everyone saw it as a priority, because in the short term the current solution still worked. What I did was prepare a simple example showing where it could fail and why a different approach would save us trouble later.

From there the conversation became much more concrete and it was easier to align the team. It was a good lesson in influencing through clarity, not just opinion.

## What project are you currently working on?

### **What the recruiter is looking for**

Your current context, level of responsibility, and the kind of technical problems you work on day to day.

### **How to structure your answer**

1. Describe the product or system.
2. Explain your role on the project.
3. Highlight technical challenges or impact.

### **Example answer**

Right now I’m working on a **[product / platform / application]** focused on **[area]**.

My role is mostly on the frontend, from feature development to decisions around component structure, API integration, and keeping the application in good shape.

Lately I’ve been more involved in **[performance / architecture / new features / refactors]**, which has been a really interesting part of the project.

## What’s the most challenging aspect of your current project?

### **What the recruiter is looking for**

Whether you’re aware of the technical challenges in your work and how you deal with them.

### **How to structure your answer**

1. Explain the challenge.
2. Say why it’s complex.
3. Describe how you’re handling it.

### **Example answer**

I’d say the most challenging part right now is continuing to evolve the product without letting complexity get out of hand.

As we add more features, integrations, and use cases, it’s easy to accumulate technical debt or less sustainable decisions.

We’re trying to address that with more attention to architecture, more consistent patterns, and some discipline in how we evolve the codebase.

## What was the hardest bug you fixed in the last 6 months?

### **What the recruiter is looking for**

Your debugging and analytical thinking.

### **How to structure your answer**

1. Context of the bug.
2. Why it was hard.
3. How you found the solution.

### **Example answer**

One of the trickiest bugs I ran into recently was inconsistent behaviour in an integration with **[API / state / event]**.

The hard part was that it didn’t happen every time, so it took a while to see a pattern. I started by trying to reproduce it in controlled conditions, checking logs, and isolating variables.

In the end I found the cause was **[race condition / synchronisation / stale state / edge case]**. It was one of those where the fix itself wasn’t the hardest part — understanding the root cause was.

## How do you approach challenges?

### **What the recruiter is looking for**

Your problem-solving process.

### **How to structure your answer**

1. Explain your approach.
2. Give a real example.
3. Share what you learned.

### **Example answer**

When a more complex challenge comes up, the first thing I try to do is not jump straight into solution mode. I try to understand the real problem first.

Then I usually break it down into smaller parts, validate assumptions, and move forward based on data rather than guesswork.

For example, in a performance situation, instead of optimising everything at once, I focused on finding exactly where the bottleneck was. That helped us focus effort in the right place and fix the problem with much more confidence.

Emotional maturity and how you deal with frustration.

## Imagine it’s your first day at the company. What would you want to work on? What would you improve?

### **What the recruiter is looking for**

Whether you’ve looked into the company’s product and how you think about **product improvement, user impact, and technical vision**.

### **How to structure your answer**

1. Show you know the company’s product.
2. Identify something that could be improved or evolved.
3. Show structured thinking about product and engineering.

### **Example answer**

On day one my focus would be understanding the product, the team, and the technical context before suggesting changes.

That said, from what I’ve seen so far, I think **[feature / product area]** has a lot of potential and is an area I’d be curious to work on. It seems like a place where small improvements could have real impact on the user experience.

But before suggesting any direction, I’d want to understand the team’s priorities, metrics, and current constraints better. I think that’s essential to propose improvements that are grounded and practical.

## Tell me about a situation where you disagreed with your manager

### **What the recruiter is looking for**

Professional maturity, communication, and respect for hierarchy without giving up critical thinking.

### **How to structure your answer**

1. Explain the context.
2. Show how you communicated your view.
3. Explain the outcome.

### **Example answer**

There was a situation where my manager and I weren't fully aligned on the priority between delivery speed and technical sustainability.

I felt that if we went too fast with that approach, we'd pay for it soon after. So I shared my view calmly, explained the risks, and tried not to turn it into a right-or-wrong issue.

In the end we found a middle ground that let us move forward without compromising the future too much. For me, the important thing in these situations is to disagree with respect and with clear reasoning.

## What would your ideal work day look like?

### **What the recruiter is looking for**

How you like to work and whether your style fits the company culture.

### **How to structure your answer**

1. Focused work.
2. Collaboration with the team.
3. Meaningful progress on the product.

### **Example answer**

For me, a good work day has a balance of focus and collaboration.

I like having blocks of time where I can go deep on a problem, but I also value time with the team — to discuss decisions, unblock things, or align on ideas.

Ultimately, an ideal day is one where I feel I moved something important forward and it had real use.

## What’s something you had to push for or insist on in a previous project?

### **What the recruiter is looking for**

Your ability to **stand behind important technical decisions**.

### **How to structure your answer**

1. Context.
2. Arguments you made.
3. Outcome.

### **Example answer**

Something I had to push for a few times was pausing to improve certain foundations of the application before building more on top.

It’s not always easy, because on the surface it can look like you’re slowing delivery. But when you explain the impact on maintenance, bugs, and future velocity, the conversation shifts.

In one of those situations we did make that improvement and it paid off in the work that followed.

## What’s the most constructive feedback you’ve received in your career?

### **What the recruiter is looking for**

Your ability to learn and self-critique.

### **How to structure your answer**

1. Describe the feedback.
2. Explain how you reacted.
3. Show how you’ve grown.

### **Example answer**

One piece of feedback that really helped was realising that sometimes I had the solution in my head but didn’t bring the team along in the best way.

So technically I might have been on the right track, but I wasn’t communicating the reasoning, trade-offs, and why behind the decision well enough.

Since then I’ve made a conscious effort to explain context better, involve people more, and make discussions clearer. I feel that’s improved how I collaborate.

## Time management

### **What the recruiter is looking for**

Organisation and productivity.

### **How to structure your answer**

1. Describe your approach.
2. Example.
3. Impact.

### **Example answer**

Something I’ve learned is not to treat big tasks as one single block.

When I break things into smaller chunks, I can estimate better, communicate progress better, and avoid feeling stuck on one topic for too long.

I also try to protect focus time for more demanding work, because I notice a real difference when I have the mental space to think properly.

## What aspects of your work do people criticise most often?

### **What the recruiter is looking for**

Self-awareness.

### **How to structure your answer**

1. Pick something real but not critical.
2. Show how you've improved.

### **Example answer**

One criticism I've had a few times is that when I'm deep in a problem I can go too far into the details.

That has an upside — it shows care — but it also pushed me to learn how to balance depth with pragmatism.

Now I try to ask more often: does this actually need this level of detail at this stage?
